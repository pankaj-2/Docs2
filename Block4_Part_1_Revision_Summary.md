## Unit 10: Market Structure and Barriers to Entry

> Explains how the number of firms, product type, and entry conditions shape competition and pricing decisions.

---

### 10.1 Introduction
- Pricing is the most critical managerial decision — too high loses customers, too low loses profit
- Price is shaped by demand, supply, number of firms, product nature, and entry possibilities

---

### 10.2 Classification of Market Structures

- **Market structure** = degree of competition in a market
- **Market power** = firm's ability to influence price (higher competition → less market power)
- **S-C-P Hypothesis**: Structure → Conduct → Performance (market structure determines firm behaviour and outcomes)

#### Four Characteristics Used to Classify Markets:

- **Number & size of sellers** — more sellers = less individual control over price
- **Number & size of buyers** — one big buyer = monopsony; few big buyers = oligopsony
- **Product differentiation** — measured by cross-elasticity; more differentiation = more pricing power
- **Conditions of entry/exit** — easy entry = more competition long-run; hard entry = firms keep power

#### Four Market Types:

```
PERFECT           MONOPOLISTIC        OLIGOPOLY         MONOPOLY
COMPETITION       COMPETITION
Many sellers      Many sellers        Few sellers       One seller
Identical         Differentiated      May/may not diff  No substitutes
Free entry        Free entry          Difficult entry   Barred entry
Price taker       Some price ctrl     Strong price ctrl Price maker
```

| Market Type          | Sellers | Concentration | Product        | Entry       |
|----------------------|---------|---------------|----------------|-------------|
| Perfect Competition  | Large   | None          | Homogeneous    | Free/Easy   |
| Monopolistic Comp.   | Large   | Low           | Close subs.    | Free/Easy   |
| Oligopoly            | Few     | Medium/High   | Homo or diff.  | Difficult   |
| Duopoly              | Two     | High          | Homo or diff.  | Very hard   |
| Monopoly             | One     | Very High     | Remote subs.   | Impossible  |

---

### 10.3 Factors Determining the Nature of Competition

#### Effect of Buyers
- **Monopsony** = only one buyer (e.g., Railways buying wagons from 6 firms)
- **Oligopsony** = few large buyers (e.g., Coal India taking 60% of explosives industry output)
- Fewer buyers → they negotiate/dictate price

#### Production Characteristics
- **Minimum Efficient Scale (MES)** = smallest output at which average cost is lowest
- High MES (e.g., steel, aircraft) → fewer firms can exist profitably → concentrated market
- Low MES (e.g., travel agents) → many small firms → competitive market
- Technology change can raise or lower MES, reshaping market structure

#### Product Characteristics
- Unique/differentiated product → firm commands premium price → more market power
- High distribution cost → competition becomes local
- Perishable products → always local competition

---

### 10.4 Barriers to Entry

- **Entry barrier** = anything that stops new firms from entering a market

#### Factors Creating Entry Conditions:
- **Legal barriers** — patents, industrial licensing (e.g., India's old licensing regime)
- **Initial capital cost** — high investment needed in coal/steel/power → few entrants
- **Vertical integration** — firm controls raw material + final product (e.g., SAIL, DMart); new entrants face high costs
- **Optimum scale** — if MES is huge, small entrants can't compete (e.g., aircraft, electricity)
- **Product differentiation** — crowded shelf space with 40+ variants blocks new entrants

#### Three Types of Entry Barriers:

```
ENTRY BARRIERS
├── Natural     → Economies of scale (e.g., power, railways)
├── Legal       → Patents, govt licenses
└── Strategic   → Deliberate actions by existing firms
```

#### Strategic Barriers (detail):
- **Entry limit pricing** — cut price below profit-max level so new entrant can't cover costs

  > **Mini Example:**
  > - Profit-max: Output = 50,000 units @ Rs.100, Cost = Rs.80 → Profit = Rs.10,00,000
  > - Entry limit: Output = 70,000 units @ Rs.90, Cost = Rs.80 → Profit = Rs.7,00,000
  > - New entrant still can't cover costs → stays out

- **Excess capacity** — signal to rivals: "We can flood the market if you enter"
- **Multiple products** — produce many variants to fill shelf space, leaving no room for entrants
- **New product development** — create substitutes for your own product → makes demand for each more elastic → new entry harder
- **Switching costs** — make it expensive for customers to switch (e.g., Microsoft Windows lock-in)

---

### 10.5 Strategic Entry Barriers — Further Discussion

- Most competitive advantages erode unless actively protected
- Three generic strategies firms use:
  1. **Sunk costs** — invest in hard-to-recover assets → raises cost for any entrant trying to displace you
  2. **Squeezing entrants** — force rivals to enter at huge scale by raising fixed costs (e.g., heavy advertising); then limit their customer access
  3. **Raising rivals' costs** — escalate advertising spend; this hurts new entrants more than incumbent (advertising is sunk for incumbent, a new burden for entrant)

- **Product differentiation advantages** → buyers pay premium; switching costs keep them locked in
- **Absolute cost advantages** → R&D, learning-by-doing, control of rare resources, patents
- **Scale-related advantages** → cost per unit falls as volume rises; small entrant = high unit cost

---

### 10.6 Pricing Analysis of Markets

- **Law of Supply**: sellers supply more at higher prices, all else equal
- Equilibrium price = where demand equals supply

#### Demand-Supply Worked Example:

| Price | Demand | Supply |
|-------|--------|--------|
| 5     | 100    | 200    |
| 4     | 120    | 180    |
| **3** | **150**| **150**|
| 2     | 200    | 110    |
| 1     | 300    | 50     |

> **Equilibrium price = 3** (Demand = Supply = 150)

#### Shifts in Equilibrium:

```
Demand increases → Price rises, Quantity rises
Demand decreases → Price falls, Quantity falls
Supply increases → Price falls, Quantity rises
Supply decreases → Price rises, Quantity falls
```

- Pricing of **multiple products**: price of one product can affect demand for another (e.g., auto firm's new model affects price of existing model)

## Unit 11: Pricing Under Perfect Competition and Pure Monopoly

> Analyzes how firms decide price and output at the two extremes of market structure — perfect competition and monopoly — both used as benchmarks.

---

### 11.2 Characteristics of Perfect Competition

- **Large number** of small buyers and sellers — no single one controls price
- **Identical/homogeneous products** — buyers don't care who they buy from
- **Free entry and exit** — no barriers; long-run profits driven to zero
- **Perfect information** — buyers and sellers know all prices
- **No transport cost advantage** — location doesn't matter
- Firms are **price-takers** (not price-makers)
- Rarely exists in real world; used as **ideal benchmark**

#### Consumer Surplus:
- = Maximum willingness to pay − Actual price paid
- Maximised under perfect competition; minimised under monopoly
- Monopoly captures consumer surplus as profit (income transfer from buyer to seller)

---

### 11.3 Profit-Maximising Output — Short Run (Perfect Competition)

- Firm's demand curve is **horizontal** (perfectly elastic) at market price
- **AR = MR = Price** (constant at all output levels)
- **Profit-max rule: Set P = MC (same as MR = MC)**

#### Three Short-Run Scenarios:

```
Price > ATC  →  Economic Profit  ✓
Price < ATC but > AVC  →  Loss, but keep producing (covers variable costs + part of fixed)
Price < AVC  →  Shut down (loss exceeds fixed costs)
```

#### Numerical Example:
> Aggregate Demand: Q = 25 − 0.5P
> Aggregate Supply: Q = 10 + 1.0P
>
> Set equal: 25 − 0.5P = 10 + 1.0P → P = **10**
> Q = 10 + 1.0(10) = **20**
>
> **Equilibrium: Price = 10, Output = 20**

---

### 11.4 Profit-Maximising Output — Long Run (Perfect Competition)

- All costs are variable in the long run
- Still maximise at **LMC = MR = Price**
- **Above-normal profits attract new entrants** → supply rises → price falls → profits vanish
- **Losses drive firms out** → supply falls → price rises → losses eliminated
- **Long-run equilibrium**: Economic profit = 0 (only normal profit earned)

#### Long-Run Worked Example:
> Price = Rs.17, Output = 240 units (where LMC = MR)
> ATC at 240 units = Rs.12
>
> Total Revenue = 17 × 240 = **Rs.4,080**
> Total Cost = 12 × 240 = **Rs.2,880**
> **Economic Profit = Rs.1,200**
>
> → New firms enter → price falls to Rs.12 → profit = 0 in long run

---

### 11.5 Characteristics of Monopoly

- **Single seller**, no close substitutes
- Entry is **blocked or very difficult**
- Firm faces **downward-sloping demand curve** (unlike flat demand in perfect competition)
- Firm is a **price-maker** (controls price by adjusting output)
- Industry demand = Firm's demand (only one firm)

---

### 11.6 Profit-Maximising Output — Monopoly

- Monopolist sets output where **MC = MR** (MR curve is below the demand/AR curve)
- Price is read from demand curve at that output → **P > MC** always
- **Super-normal profits persist even in the long run** (entry is blocked)

```
D (AR)
 \
  \      ← Monopoly price (P) read here
   \
    \   MC
     \ /
      X ← Profit-max point: MC = MR
     / \
    /   MR (below AR)
```

#### Lerner Index (measure of monopoly power):

> **µ = (P − MC) / P = 1/e**
> where e = price elasticity of demand

- Higher µ → more monopoly power
- Less elastic demand → more power (e.g., life-saving medicines, petroleum)

#### Mini Example:
> P = Rs.100, MC = Rs.60
> µ = (100 − 60) / 100 = **0.4** → moderate monopoly power

---

### 11.7 Welfare: Perfect Competition vs Monopoly

```
PERFECT COMPETITION          MONOPOLY
P = MC                       P > MC
Normal profit (long run)     Super-normal profit (long run)
Max consumer surplus         Low consumer surplus
Efficient output             Restricted output
No deadweight loss           Deadweight loss (BCD area)
```

- **Deadweight loss** = value lost to society due to lower output under monopoly (no one gains this — it's pure loss)
- **Income transfer**: rectangle of profit that was consumer surplus now goes to the monopolist
- **Government regulates monopolies** to restore competitive outcomes (e.g., TRAI for telecom, electricity regulators)

---

### 11.8 Implications for Managerial Decision-Making

- Perfect competition and monopoly are **benchmarks** — real markets fall between them
- Key lesson: understand **price elasticity of demand** and **marginal cost** before pricing
- Perfect competition → match market price; focus on cost control
- Monopoly → set MR = MC; focus on restricting entry and maximising profit
- Real pricing decisions blend both insights (e.g., Pepsi's 12-oz bottle at same price as Coke's 6-oz = elastic demand strategy)
