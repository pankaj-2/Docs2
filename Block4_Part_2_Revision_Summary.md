## Chapter 12: Pricing Under Monopolistic and Oligopolistic Competition

**Overview:** Covers two real-world market structures — monopolistic competition (many sellers, differentiated products) and oligopoly (few sellers, interdependent decisions) — and how firms set prices in each.

---

### 12.1 Introduction
- Pricing is the most important firm decision; market structure determines how much pricing power a firm has.
- Monopolistic competition and oligopoly lie between the two extremes of perfect competition and monopoly.

---

### 12.2 Monopolistic Competition
- **Definition (Joe Bain):** Many sellers selling differentiated but close-substitute products.
- **Edward Chamberlin** developed the model; observed products differ in packaging, fragrance, branding, etc.
- **Key features:**
  - Large number of sellers — each acts independently
  - Large number of buyers — each acts independently
  - Sufficient buyer knowledge about products
  - Differentiated products (marginal differences, e.g., toothpaste)
  - Free entry and exit

#### Nature of Demand Curve
- **Less than perfectly elastic** — product differentiation means rivals are close but not perfect substitutes
- **Downward sloping** — varied consumer tastes mean the firm has some price control

---

### 12.3 Price & Output Determination — Short Run
- Each firm has some monopoly power → sets its own price.
- Products are similar but NOT identical → no single unique price exists.
- Firm maximises profit where **MR = MC** → produces output Q at price P.
- Short-run **economic (super-normal) profit is possible**.

```
Price
  |        MC
  |       /
P |--A   /   ATC
  |   \ /  /
R |----Q--/------  AR (Demand)
  |       MR
  |___________________
             Quantity

Profit = Area PAQR (shaded rectangle above ATC)
```

---

### 12.4 Price & Output Determination — Long Run
- Super-normal profits attract **new entrants** → more substitutes → demand curve shifts DOWN for existing firms.
- Entry continues until **P = LRAC** (zero economic profit).
- Difference from perfect competition: tangency point is on the **downward-sloping** part of LRAC (NOT at minimum).
- **Excess capacity** exists — firms produce less than their minimum average cost output.
- Higher price is justified by **product variety** available to consumers.

```
Long-Run Equilibrium:

Price
  |    LRMC
  |   /
P |--*  ATC(LRAC)
  |   \ \
  |    AR (tangent to LRAC at P)
  |      MR
  |___________________
       Q        Quantity

At Q: P = LRAC, MR = LRMC → Zero economic profit
```

---

### 12.5 Oligopolistic Competition

#### Definition & Features
- **Few sellers** of a homogeneous or differentiated product.
- **Duopoly** = only two sellers.
- **Pure oligopoly** = homogeneous product (steel, aluminium).
- **Differentiated oligopoly** = differentiated product (cars, cigarettes).
- Entry is possible but **not easy** — high barriers exist.
- Key feature: **mutual interdependence** — each firm's action affects others.

#### Sources / Barriers to Entry
- Economies of scale → few firms serve the whole market
- Huge capital investment needed
- Patent ownership
- Loyal customer base hard to replicate
- Control of key raw materials
- Government franchise granted to few firms
- **Limit pricing** — existing firms price low to block new entrants

#### Types of Oligopolist Behaviour
```
Oligopolists
    |
    |--- Cooperative: follows rivals' price moves
    |--- Non-cooperative: does NOT follow rivals' moves
         (e.g., keeps price low when rival raises price)
```

---

### Price Rigidity: Kinked Demand Curve (Sweezy Model)
- Explains why prices tend to **stay stable** in oligopoly markets.
- **Asymmetric behaviour:**
  - If firm RAISES price → rivals do NOT follow → firm loses many customers (elastic response)
  - If firm LOWERS price → rivals DO follow → firm gains few customers (inelastic response)
- This creates a **kink** at the current price P₁.

```
Price
  |  D (elastic above kink)
  | \
P₁|--KINK
  |    \  (inelastic below kink)
  |     D
  |___________________
  Q₁         Quantity

MR has a DISCONTINUITY (gap AB) at the kink.
Even if MC shifts, as long as it stays in the gap AB,
price stays at P₁ → Price Rigidity
```

---

### Price Competition: Cartels & Collusion

#### Cartel
- **Definition:** Market-sharing + price-fixing arrangement among firms to limit competition.
- **Explicit cartel:** Members openly agree on price and output quotas.
- **Implicit cartel:** Secret collusion.
- **OPEC** is the classic textbook example (raised oil prices from <$3 to >$30/barrel in the 1970s).
- Cartel maximises profit by acting as a **monopoly** (set MR = MC of the cartel).

**Mini Example — Cartel Output Allocation:**
```
Cartel MC (MCc) = horizontal sum of all members' MC curves
Set MR = MCc → get cartel price P and total output Q
Allocate output so each firm's MC = MCc
```

**Why cartels are unstable:**
- Each member has an incentive to **cheat** (produce more than quota) because P > MC.
- If all cheat → market-sharing breaks down → cartel collapses.

---

### Game Theory: Prisoner's Dilemma
- Shows why **collusion is hard to sustain** when each firm acts in self-interest.

```
                    FIRM B
                  High P    Low P
FIRM A  High P  | 10,10  |  2,15 |
        Low P   | 15,2   |  5,5  |

(Numbers = profits)
Both choose Low P (dominant strategy) → both worse off.
Best joint outcome = both High P, but individually each defects.
```

- **Tit-for-tat strategy (Axelrod):** Cooperate first, then mirror rival's last move → best way to sustain cooperation in repeated games.

---

### Price Leadership
- One firm sets price; others follow — avoids destructive price wars.

#### Two Forms:
| Type | Who Leads | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Dominant** | Largest firm | Can start price war if others don't comply — like monopoly |
| **Barometric** | Firm with best reputation | No power to force others; others follow only if they trust the leader |

- Indian example: Airlines (dominant) and Automobiles (barometric).

## Chapter 13: Pricing Strategies

**Overview:** Covers advanced pricing techniques firms use to extract maximum revenue — including price discrimination, bundling, two-part tariffs, and transfer pricing.

---

### 13.2 Concentration Ratios, Herfindahl Index & Contestable Markets

#### Concentration Ratio
- Measures % of total industry sales held by the 4/8/12 largest firms.
- 4-firm ratio near 100 → clearly oligopolistic.

#### Herfindahl Index (H)
- **Formula:** H = Σ (market share of each firm)²
- Higher H = more concentrated market.

**Mini Example:**
```
2 firms: one with 90%, one with 10%
H = 90² + 10² = 8100 + 100 = 8,200  (highly concentrated)

4 equal firms (25% each):
H = 4 × 25² = 4 × 625 = 2,500

100 equal firms (1% each):
H = 100 × 1² = 100  (perfectly competitive)
```

#### Contestable Markets
- Even a monopoly behaves competitively if:
  - Entry is **absolutely free** (same costs as existing firms)
  - Exit is **entirely costless** (no sunk costs)
- Firms price at average cost → zero economic profit.

---

### 13.3 Price Discrimination
- **Definition:** Charging different prices to different buyers for the same product.
- Requires **monopoly power** — impossible in perfect competition.
- Requires **market segmentation** — buyers cannot resell between segments.

```
Three Degrees of Price Discrimination:

1st Degree → Different price for EACH unit (willingness to pay)
             Example: specialist doctor charges by patient's ability to pay

2nd Degree → Different price for different BLOCKS of units (volume)
             Example: electricity slab rates; buy 2 get 1 cheaper

3rd Degree → Different price for different GROUPS of buyers
             Example: student vs. business class airline tickets
```

#### First Degree (Perfect Price Discrimination)
- Seller captures **entire consumer surplus**.
- Best achieved via **sealed bid auctions** (art, land rights).
- Not practical for mass-produced goods.

#### Second Degree (Multipart / Block Pricing)
- First block of units sold at higher price P₁; next block at lower P₂.
- Firm captures part of consumer surplus (area P₁BCP₂ in graph).
- Also includes **two-tier tariffs** (flat fee + usage fee).

**Mini Example:**
```
P₁ = ₹2000 for 1st shirt
P₂ = ₹1000 for 2nd shirt
→ Seller captures extra ₹1000 from high-value buyer
```

#### Third Degree (Market Segmentation)
- Divide market into segments with **different price elasticities**.
- Charge **higher price** to less price-sensitive group (business travellers).
- Charge **lower price** to more price-sensitive group (students, senior citizens).
- Risk: arbitrage (low-price buyer resells to high-price buyer) → seller must prevent this.

---

### 13.4 Peak Load Pricing
- A form of **3rd-degree price discrimination** based on TIME.
- Charge higher price during **peak (inelastic) period** and lower during **off-peak (elastic) period**.
- Example: Hill station hotels charge more in summer, less in off-season; Delhi Metro higher fares in rush hour.

---

### 13.5 Bundling
- **Definition:** Selling two or more products together at a single combined price.

```
Three Types:

Pure Bundling  → Products sold ONLY as a bundle (not separately)
                 Example: Microsoft Office 365

Mixed Bundling → Products sold BOTH separately AND as a bundle
                 Example: Fast food meal deal vs. individual items

Tying          → Buying main product forces you to buy a tied product
                 Example: Mach3 razor (main) + cartridges (tied)
```

- Bundling benefits: reduces search costs, lower transaction costs, may lower distribution costs.
- Risk: can be anti-competitive (Microsoft + Internet Explorer case).

---

### 13.6 Two-Part Tariffs
- **Definition:** Buyer pays a **fixed entry fee** + a **per-unit usage fee**.
- Entry fee captures consumer surplus; usage fee = price per unit.
- Example: Mobile postpaid plan (monthly fixed charge + per-call charge); theme parks (entry ticket + ride charges).

**Mini Example (Theme Park):**
```
Monopoly price per ride = $6 → tourist takes 4 rides → Revenue = $24
Consumer surplus at $6 = $8 (triangle above price line)

Two-part tariff option:
  Entry fee = $8  (captures surplus)
  + $6 per ride × 4 rides = $24
  Total revenue = $32  (more than $24)

Best option: Set price per ride = $0, entry fee = full consumer surplus
  10 rides × average value ≈ $50 entry fee (but MC increases, so optimise)
```

---

### 13.7 Pricing of Joint Products
- Joint products = two goods produced together in **fixed proportions** (e.g., leather shoes + handbags).
- Cannot allocate total cost to one product alone — cost belongs to the **product package**.
- Profit-maximising rule: Set **MR_Total = MC_package**, then find individual prices from each product's demand curve.

**Mini Example:**
```
Shoes:   P = 60 – Q,   MR = 60 – 2Q
Handbags: P = 80 – 2Q, MR = 80 – 4Q
MC (package) = 30 + 5Q

Total MR = (60–2Q) + (80–4Q) = 140 – 6Q

Set MR_T = MC:
140 – 6Q = 30 + 5Q
110 = 11Q
Q = 10

Prices: Shoes = 60–10 = $50; Handbags = 80–20 = $60
Check: MR for each = 40 > 0 ✓ → Prices are profit-maximising
```

---

### 13.8 Transfer Pricing
- **Definition:** Price set for goods/services traded between divisions or subsidiaries of the same company.
- Example: Branch A sells goods to Branch B → price charged = transfer price.
- Purpose: separate profit measurement per division; guides internal resource allocation.
- **OECD governs** transfer pricing for multinationals to prevent tax avoidance.
- **Arm's Length Principle (ALP):** Treat related-party transactions as if between independent parties (at fair market price).

#### Methods:
```
Traditional Transaction Methods:
  - Comparable Uncontrolled Price (CUP): compare controlled vs. uncontrolled transaction prices
  - Resale Price Method: work backward from selling price minus margin
  - Cost Plus Method: add markup to supplier's total cost

Transactional Profit Methods:
  - Comparable Profits Method: base on net profit of controlled transaction
  - Profit Split Method: split combined profits as unrelated parties would
```

## Quick Comparison: Monopolistic Competition vs. Oligopoly

```
Feature              | Monopolistic Comp.     | Oligopoly
---------------------|------------------------|---------------------------
No. of sellers       | Many                   | Few
Product type         | Differentiated         | Homogeneous or Diff.
Entry barriers       | Low (free entry/exit)  | High
Pricing power        | Some (due to diff.)    | Significant
Key behaviour        | Independent decisions  | Mutual interdependence
Long-run profit      | Zero (P = AC)          | Can be positive (barriers)
Key model            | Chamberlin             | Sweezy (kinked demand)
```
