## Chapter 1: Scope of Managerial Economics

**Overview:** Managerial Economics applies microeconomic tools to real business decisions. It bridges pure economic theory and day-to-day managerial problems like pricing, production, and resource allocation.

### 1.1 Introduction
- Economics splits into **Macroeconomics** (big picture: GDP, inflation, unemployment) and **Microeconomics** (firms & individuals).
- Managerial Economics = **applied microeconomics** focused on firm-level decisions.
- Managers must understand economic terms (GDP growth, RBI money supply, fiscal deficit) because they directly affect demand and costs.

### 1.2 Fundamental Nature of Managerial Economics
- **Management** = coordinating people to achieve group goals (Koontz & O'Donell).
- **Economics** = study of allocating scarce resources among unlimited wants.
- Scarcity forces every economy to answer 3 core questions:

```
         SCARCITY
        /    |    \
       /     |     \
What to   How to   For whom
produce?  produce?  to produce?
```

- These 3 questions are solved by: (1) markets alone, (2) government alone, or (3) a mix — most real economies use the mix.
- **Market efficiency:** At supply = demand, benefit of one more unit = cost of producing it → "efficient" outcome.
- **4 types of market failure:** Monopoly, Public Goods, Externalities, Information problems.

### 1.3 Scope of Managerial Economics
- Managerial Economics = economics applied to **"problems of choice"** inside a firm.
- **4 main problem areas:**
  - **Resource Allocation** — using scarce inputs optimally (production planning, transport).
  - **Inventory & Queuing** — deciding optimal stock levels; when to add machines/labour.
  - **Pricing** — choosing the right price method for products.
  - **Investment** — allocating resources over time (new plants, funding sources).
- Core subjects studied: firm objectives, demand analysis, production & cost, pricing, break-even, capital budgeting, competition.

### 1.4 Appropriate Definitions

| Scholar | Definition (paraphrased) |
|---|---|
| McNair & Meriam | Using economic thinking to analyse business situations |
| Evan J Douglas | Applying economic principles to decision-making under uncertainty |
| Spencer & Siegelman | Integrating economic theory with business practice for planning |
| Hailstones & Rothwel | Applying economic theory to business firms and institutions |

- **Common thread:** Use a structured framework → better decisions → maximise firm objectives.

### 1.5 Managerial Economics and Other Disciplines
- **Microeconomics** — root source; price theory, demand, market structure.
- **Macroeconomics** — useful mainly for national income forecasting → input for demand forecasting.
- **Operations Research** — linear programming helps optimise product mix, scheduling.
- **Decision Theory** — handles uncertainty; recognises multiple goals (unlike single-goal economics).
- **Statistics** — empirical testing; demand & cost estimation.
- **Management Accounting** — accounting data is the "language of business"; closely linked.

### 1.6 Economic Analysis
- Economic activity = constantly matching limited means to unlimited ends.
- **Types of analysis used:**
  - **Micro vs Macro** — single entity vs. whole economy.
  - **Partial vs General Equilibrium** — one market at a time vs. all markets together.
  - **Static / Comparative Static / Dynamic** — no change / one-time change / ongoing changes over time.
  - **Positive vs Normative** — "what is" (fact) vs. "what should be" (value judgement).

> **Example — Positive vs Normative:**
> - Positive: "CEOs earn 15× more than the lowest-paid employee." (fact)
> - Normative: "CEOs should earn only 4–5× more." (opinion/value)

### 1.7 Basic Characteristics: Decision-Making
- Managerial Economics is the **link** between traditional economics and decision science.

```
Decision Problem
      |
      v
Traditional Economics  +  Decision Science Tools
             \                /
              v              v
         MANAGERIAL ECONOMICS
                  |
                  v
     Optimal Solution to Business Problems
```

- **Key characteristics of Managerial Economics:**
  - Concerned with economic-nature decisions.
  - Micro-economic in character.
  - Uses "theory of the firm."
  - Goal-oriented and prescriptive.
  - Both conceptual (theory) and metrical (measurement).

## Chapter 2: The Firm — Stakeholders, Objectives and Decision Issues

**Overview:** This chapter explains why firms exist, what goals they pursue, and the key economic principles (incremental, equi-marginal, discounting, opportunity cost) that guide managerial decisions.

### 2.1 Introduction: The Firm
- A firm buys inputs → transforms them → sells outputs.
- Firms exist because **internal transactions are cheaper** than market transactions (saves transaction costs).
- Firm size is limited: as the firm grows, internal costs eventually exceed market costs.
- **4 factors of production (FOP)** and their rewards:

| Factor | Reward |
|---|---|
| Land | Rent |
| Capital | Interest |
| Labour | Wages |
| Entrepreneur | Profit |

- **Circular flow:**
```
Consumers ──(buy goods)──> Product Market ──> Firms
   ^                                              |
   |←── Income (wages/rent) ── Factor Market <───|
```

### 2.2 Objective of the Firm: Accounting vs Economic Profit

- **Accounting Profit** = Revenue − Explicit Costs
- **Economic Profit** = Revenue − Explicit Costs − **Implicit Costs**
- **Implicit costs** = foregone opportunities (e.g. interest you could have earned, salary you gave up).

> **Mini Example:**
>
> | Item | Amount (₹) |
> |---|---|
> | Sales | 90,000 |
> | Cost of goods + expenses | 70,000 |
> | **Accounting Profit** | **20,000** |
> | Less: Opportunity cost of capital (10% on ₹1 lakh) | 10,000 |
> | Less: Foregone MBA salary | 35,000 |
> | **Economic Profit** | **−25,000 (loss!)** |
>
> → The business looks profitable to an accountant but is an **economic loss**.

- **Profit's 2 roles in a free market:**
  1. Signal to enter/exit an industry.
  2. Reward for entrepreneurship and risk-taking.

### 2.3 Value Maximization
- Modern firms focus on **long-term value**, not just short-term profit.
- **Value of the firm** = Present Value of all future profits:

```
         π₁         π₂               πₙ
PV(π) = ──── + ──────── + ... + ────────
        (1+r)   (1+r)²           (1+r)ⁿ
```
Where π = profit, r = discount rate, n = time period.

- Marketing → raises TR; Operations → lowers TC; Finance → lowers r. All increase firm value.

### 2.4 Alternative Objectives of Firms

| Economist | Objective |
|---|---|
| Baumol | Maximise **sales revenue** (manager pay linked to sales, not profit) |
| Robin Marris | Maximise **firm growth rate** (managers & owners both want bigger firm) |
| Oliver Williamson | Maximise **manager's utility** (salary, power, status) subject to minimum profit |
| Cyert & March | **Satisficing** — meet aspiration levels across production, sales, inventory, profit |

### 2.5 Goals of Real World Firms
- Large firms have many management levels → managers may chase personal goals.
- **Principal-Agent Problem:** Owners (principals) hire managers (agents) who may not act in owners' interest.
- **Solution:** Tie manager pay to firm performance — bonuses, **stock options**.
- Stock options make managers act like owners (they benefit only if share price rises).

### 2.6 Firm's Constraints
- **Resource** — limited skilled workers, scarce raw materials.
- **Legal** — environmental, employment, waste disposal laws.
- **Moral** — socially accepted standards (not illegal, but expected).
- **Contractual** — existing leases, union contracts.

### 2.7 The Incremental Concept
- **Incremental Cost (IC)** = change in total cost due to a decision.
- **Incremental Revenue (IR)** = change in total revenue due to a decision.
- **Decision rule: Accept if IR > IC.**

> **Mini Example:**
>
> New order offers IR = ₹2,000.
>
> | | Full Cost | Incremental Cost |
> |---|---|---|
> | Labour | ₹600 | ₹400 |
> | Materials | ₹800 | ₹800 |
> | Overheads | ₹720 | ₹200 |
> | Total | ₹2,400 | ₹1,400 |
>
> Full cost → appears as ₹400 loss. Incremental view → **₹600 profit**. Accept the order!

- Incrementalism is the **general** form; marginalism is a specific (unit-by-unit) form.

### 2.8 The Equi-Marginal Principle
- Allocate resources so that **marginal benefit ÷ marginal cost is equal** across all uses.
- Consumer rule: MU₁/MC₁ = MU₂/MC₂ = ... = MUₙ/MCₙ
- Producer rule: MRP₁/MC₁ = MRP₂/MC₂ = ... = MRPₙ/MCₙ

> **Mini Example (from text):**
> Budget for 6 units across items A, B, C (each costs the same):
>
> | Units | A | B | C |
> |---|---|---|---|
> | 1 | 10 | 9 | 8 |
> | 2 | 9 | 8 | 7 |
> | 3 | **8** | 7 | 6 |
> | 4 | 7 | **8→** | 5 |
>
> Optimal: **3A + 2B + 1C** → all give MU = 8. Total utility maximised.

### 2.9 The Discounting Principle
- A rupee today is worth **more** than a rupee in the future (time value of money).
- **Present Value formula:**
```
        Rₙ
PV = ────────
      (1+i)ⁿ
```
Where Rₙ = future amount, i = interest rate, n = number of years.

> **Mini Example:**
> PV of ₹100 receivable in 1 year at 5% interest:
> PV = 100 ÷ 1.05 = **₹95.24**
>
> PV of ₹100 in 2 years:
> PV = 100 ÷ (1.05)² = **₹90.70**

- **Annuity** = equal payments each period (e.g. home loan EMI).
- **Perpetuity** = annuity that goes on forever.

### 2.10 The Opportunity Cost Principle
- Opportunity cost = value of the **next best alternative** you give up.
- All economic costing uses the opportunity cost principle.
- Examples:
  - Farmer growing wheat → opportunity cost = potatoes not grown.
  - Holding ₹1000 cash → opportunity cost = 10% interest not earned.

- **Production Possibility Curve (PPC)** shows opportunity costs at the economy level:
```
Butter
  |A
  |  \
  |B   \
  |  C  \   ← Concave shape = increasing opportunity cost
  |    D \
  |      E\
  +─────────→ Guns
```
- Curve is **concave** because factors are not equally efficient in all uses (specificity).
- Moving along the curve: more guns = more butter sacrificed (increasing opportunity cost).

### 2.11 The Invisible Hand
- Adam Smith's concept: each person/firm acting in **self-interest** accidentally benefits the whole economy.
- Producers → profit motive → produce what consumers want → at lowest cost.
- No central planner needed; market forces solve the 3 core economic questions automatically.
